A brief history about
how DevOps got its name


       

10 Deploys per Day: Dev and Ops Cooperation at Flickr.

Back in 2007: While consulting on a data center migration for the Belgium government, sys admin Patrick Debois becomes frustrated by conflicts between developers and operators. (click)

August 2008: At the Agile Conference in Toronto, software developer Andrew Shafer posts notice of a “birds of a feather” session entitled “Agile Infrastructure.” Exactly one person attends: Patrick Debois. Andrew skips his own session since there was no interest in his topic! Later, Debois tracks down Shafer. Based on their talk, they form the Agile Systems Administration Group.

Another important event. At the O’Reilly Velocity 09 conference, John Allspaw and Paul Hammond gave their now legendary talk, “10 Deploys per Day: Dev and Ops Cooperation at Flickr.” Debois wasn’t there, and he tweets that he is very sorry he isn’t there. Paul Nasrat tweets back, “Why not organize your own Velocity event in Belgium?”

October 2009: And he does exactly that—but first, he needs a name. He takes the first three letters of development and operations, adds the word “days,” and calls it DevOpsDays.

 

The conference doors open on October 30 to an impressive collection of developers, system administrators, toolsmiths, and others. When the conference ends, the ongoing discussions move to Twitter. To create a memorable hashtag, Debois shortens the name to #DevOps. And the movement has been known as DevOps ever since.

Definition #2

Developers carry beepers. Kent Beck at a DevOps Norway Meetup in 2013

  • Kent Beck once gave me his definition of DevOps at a DevOps Meetup here in Oslo, "Developers carry beepers".
  • And he is absolutely right. It must be the ones that develop the systems that must watch over them.
  • They understand the systems a lot better than anyone else, they understand the risks, and they are better off fixing problems when they occur.
  • It also creates incentives to build things that don't break.

    • You build it, you run it!

    Werner Vogels, CTO Amazon
    A Conversation with Werner Vogels, Queue, June 30th, 2006

    If you let the team take responsibility for the system as a whole you make sure that everything in the system is well understood.

    The artificial boundry between operations and development has to go. The team must be responsible for the whole.

    Therefore we are also on call. And we use the pagerduty app to buzz us if something unexpected happens in production.

    This of course also reduces handovers, misunderstandings and improves velocity.
