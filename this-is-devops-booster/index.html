A brief history about
how DevOps got its name

I'm going to start off with a brief history about how DevOps got its name.

       

10 Deploys per Day: Dev and Ops Cooperation at Flickr.

Back in 2007: Sysadmin Patrick Debois becomes frustrated by conflicts between developers and operators while consulting on a data center migration for the Belgium government. (click)

August 2008: At the Agile Conference in Toronto, software developer (click) Andrew Shafer proposes an (click) open space session entitled “Agile Infrastructure.” Exactly one person attends: Patrick Debois. Andrew skips his own session since there was no interest in his topic! Later, Debois tracks down Shafer. Based on their talk, they form the Agile Systems Administration Group.

(click) Another important event. At the O’Reilly Velocity 09 conference, John Allspaw and Paul Hammond gave their now legendary talk, “10 Deploys per Day: Dev and Ops Cooperation at Flickr.” Debois wasn’t there, and he tweets that he is very sorry he isn’t there. Paul Nasrat tweets back, “Why not organize your own Velocity event in Belgium?”

October 2009: He does exactly that — but first, he needs a name. He takes the first three letters of development and operations, adds the word “days,” and calls it DevOpsDays.

 

The conference doors open on October 30th to an impressive collection of developers, system administrators, toolsmiths, and others. When the conference ends, the ongoing discussions move to Twitter. To create a memorable hashtag, Debois shortens the name to...

#devops

... #DevOps. And the movement has been known as DevOps ever since.

 

Since then there has been over 168 Devopsdays, and this year it has been 52 alone. And there's still 5 more later this year. That's more than once a week! The latest one was here in Oslo.

What is DevOps?

So, what is DevOps?

Definition

…it is getting developers and operations folk to work closely
together to benefit the business.

Barton George

An idiot’s guide to DevOps, Sept 30th, 2013
Image by newwavegurly licensed by CC BY-NC-ND 2.0
Its original meaning is this. It's about getting two diciplines that traditionally has been very separate to work together.

We have no Ops!

But high performing teams don't have ops anymore!

NoOps != No Operations

But No Ops does not mean that there's no more operations.

Making s**t work in production (2007)

 

So we needed to learn this stuff. This book was written before devops. But it is DevOps. It's one of the best books I've read and it's basically about making shit work in production.

Developers vs. Operators
Engineers/Technologists

There's a new breed in town. I don't know what to call it, but engineer or technologist are good candidates. They do both ops and development. Making shit work in production.

DevOps ≈ Continuous Delivery (2010)

...a software development discipline where you build software in such a way that the software can be released to production at any time.

Martin Fowler

Continuous delivery is about putting the release schedule in the hands of the business, not in the hands of IT.

Jez Humble
  • DevOps is strongly related to continuous delivery
  • Our software must be ready for production at any time.
  • We need to do this to support how business wants to work.
  • It's all about putting the release schedule in the hands of the business, not in the hands of IT.
  • To do that we must also control operations. There's no time for hand-overs, and everything needs to be automated so that we can put our stuff into production at the touch of a button

Continuous Delivery ♥ DevOps

Continuous delivery loves DevOps. Because it includes the IT systems as a whole, increases quality and enhances velocity.

DevOps ≈ Lean (2013)

...something called “DevOps.” Maybe everyone ... is a form of DevOps, but I suspect it’s something much more than that. It’s Product Management, Development, IT Operations, and even Information Security all working together and supporting one another.

Gene Kim, The Phoenix Project: A Novel About IT, DevOps, and Helping Your Business Win
  • DevOps is described as Lean in the famous book "The Phoenix Project" by Gene Kim.
  • That kick-started a new understanding of devops. Namely that it includes many more roles than just devs and ops.

Even UX and design has embraced the DevOps mindset. Julia and Maria have the balls to test less and iterate in production on 90% designs. (Julia og Maria)

Lean ♥ DevOps

Lean loves DevOps. Because it enhances flow.
