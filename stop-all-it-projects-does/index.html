So you get a lot of money
Set your scope
Staff up with peolple who haven't worked together before.
Press start, and you have already decided when to press stop. Because then all the money is spent. Probably more money than you expected.

And then you start

And then...Do Agile!

And finally it's time to hand everything over to operations or the line.

And theeeeen you can get some value from what ever you've been creating.

The ability to produce value early and continuously is gone, and when the project is done chances are that you have failed miserably on creating value.

But my project is Agile!

OK, some have improved.

Many arganizations use Agile methodologies when they run their projects.

They have more slack in their scope and it is possible to change direction as you go.

Very well, but it's not enough.

Agile is not enough!

The problem doesn't sit in Agile as such, but outside.

With Agile you can produce working software (in theory) fast and continuously.

But everything outside of your project has to be able to receive what is produced at the same pace.

The line must be able to absorb what is produced. E. g. Operations.

How the software works in production after it's been handed over is unfortunately seldomn the Agile teams' responsibility.

“You build it, you run it”
Werner Vogels (CTO i Amazon)

You build it, you run it!

If you use the line to create products or services instead you can let the team take responsibility from day one.

You push maintenance and operations to the very start of the development.

They who create the software must be responsible from conception to when the software dies.

Challenges with project as a way of doing work

  • it's hard to produce value continuously
  • conflict between project goal and organizational goals
  • large gap between the project and the line
  • short-term financing
  • challenges with handovers and knowledge transfer
  • difficult to scale

Brooks' law

Adding manpower to a late
software project makes it later
Fred Brooks, The Mythical Man-Month, 1975

This is true for projects. You can't simply increase the budget and hire more people.

With continuous product development in _the line_ "to late" is never a thing. There you can adjust staffing continuously based on prioritization and business needs.

Conway's law

Organizations which design systems ... are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations
M. Conway, How Do Committees Invent, 1968

I will talk a little bit about organization.

Melvin Conway said in 1968 that your systems will mirror your organization.

This is probably why we have so many monoliths everywhere.

Projects create monoliths becaus they are separate from the rest of the organization.

There is a homomorphism from the linear graph of a system to the linear graph of its organization.

Demystifying Conway's Law

Microservices allow organizations to align the architecture of their systems to the structure of their teams.
Sam Newman, Demystifying Conway’s Law, 2015

A better alternative is to create feature teams aligned with business areas.

Netflix and Amazon has an organization where small teams have responsibility for a small part of the over all system.

The same with Spotify.

As we can see Conway has influenced one of the big hypes today – microservices. This is James Lewis and Martin Fowlers illustration of microservices. (flip back)

Microservices have no precise definition, e.g. size.

Some common characteristica

  • deployed independent from each other
  • automated deployment
  • decentralized and must be orchestrated.
  • talk to each other via well defined APIs
  • organized around business capabilities
    • the characteristica which makes service design flexible
    • but not without a flexible organization

Conway's Corollary

Organizational flexibility
is important to effective design
M. Conway, How Do Committees Invent, 1968
Conway's Corollary. The flexibility of the organization will affect how it designs things. Also the architecture of the systems they produce.

Henrik Knibergs from Spotify explains how they keep their teams as autonomous as possible.

Autonomous teams are teams who can solve all tasks which is necessary to develop a product without interference from outside or the need to contact people external to the team.

The different teams have responsibility for the entire lifecycle of their product.

They are independent from each other so they can be pushed to production faster.

This is not a project organisation. It is line work!

Every team work on their business area.

Spotify used to be a monolith, but now each team has the responsibility for the player, the playlist, artist information, mobile app and so on.

The wholy trinity. The modern IT-organization.

The customer, the users and a cross functional team that can solve everything from development to operations and maintenance.

And it scales

Autonomous teams focusing on their part. Each team can solve everything needed to develop their business area without dependencies to external partys outside the team.

If you think about it. Decreasing communication is a good thing.

The organizations vision is like a guiding star for everything the business is doing as a whole.

From CapEx to OpEx

  • CapEx = expenditures creating future benefits
  • OpEx = expenses incurred in the course of ordinary business

CapEx is guessing how much it will cost

OpEx is “business as usual”

The alternative

Continuous product development in the line.

Continuous ...

  • realization of value
  • adjustments of the initiatives against business goals
  • involvement of the organization as a whole
  • financing
  • communication
  • knowledge building instead of knowledge transfer
  • scaling by re-prioritizing finances

Criterions for successful agile “projects”

  • ongoing hours – not fixed price
  • on time and on budget is less important
  • measure realization of value continuously
  • flexible scope
  • frequent delivery

Pieces of advice:

  • Experiment with new ideas in the line
  • Put together autonomous teams with all the skills necessary to perform the experiments
  • Make sure the experiments can be measured
  • Business must cooperate closely with the team
  • Everyone must cooperate closely with users
  • Engineers must be responsible from conception to end of life
  • The team must be on pager duty!
  • Deliver software continuously to production from day one
  • Do not implement the proposed changes as a project
